## PHASE 0: MINDSET BEFORE YOU START

### The Golden Rules of Selling a Tech Platform

1. **Never sell from desperation.** Buyers can smell it. If you need cash urgently, take a bridge loan — don't sell at a 50% discount.
2. **Create competition.** One buyer = their price. Three buyers = your price. Always run a competitive process.
3. **Data beats claims.** Every number you present must be verifiable. Screenshots, Stripe exports, database counts.
4. **Protect the IP before you disclose.** NDA first. Always. No exceptions.
5. **The technical sophistication sells the price.** Walk every buyer through what this engine does. Most will not understand it — that's your job to explain it simply and powerfully.
6. **Know your walk-away number before you start.** Set it privately, tell no one. Do not negotiate against yourself.

## PHASE 1: PREPARATION (WEEKS 1–4)

### Step 1.1 — Clean Up the Platform

Before any buyer sees anything, the platform must be in its best possible state:

- [ ] All features work — test every page, every button, every flow
- [ ] No broken pages, no 500 errors, no console errors
- [ ] Mobile-responsive (check on iPhone Safari and Android Chrome)
- [ ] The track record page is populated with real historical picks (if available)
- [ ] Stripe is in live mode (or clearly documented as test mode with explanation)
- [ ] All legal disclaimers are present on every user-facing page
- [ ] Privacy Policy and Terms of Service are published and up to date

### Step 1.2 — Organize Your Data Room

Use this folder (the investor-dataroom directory) as your data room. Add these real documents:

- [ ] Actual subscriber count (screenshot from Stripe)
- [ ] Actual MRR (Stripe dashboard screenshot)
- [ ] Actual user count (DB query screenshot)
- [ ] API cost history (OpenAI dashboard)
- [ ] Hosting cost history (Replit invoice)
- [ ] Any real testimonials or user feedback
- [ ] Your development cost (hours invested × estimated hourly rate)

### Step 1.3 — Protect Your IP Legally

**Before talking to any buyer:**

1. **File trademark applications** for your key names with USPTO:
   - SORS MAXIMA (primary brand)
   - Life Changer Ticket™
   - Quantum Fusion Engine™
   - Cost: ~$350/mark via TEAS Plus (teas.uspto.gov)
   - Time: 8–12 months to approve, but "intent to use" filing protects you from Day 1

2. **Copyright the codebase:**
   - Add a copyright notice in your repository: `Copyright © 2026 [Your Name]. All rights reserved.`
   - Register with the US Copyright Office (copyright.gov) — $65 for software
   - This creates a legal record of ownership

3. **Document the creation date:**
   - GitHub commit history is timestamped — preserve it
   - Keep Replit deployment history

4. **Consult a tech M&A attorney:**
   - Not optional if you're expecting $1M+
   - Cost: $2,000–$5,000 for a full deal review
   - Find one who specializes in SaaS/software transactions
   - Directories: Martindale, Avvo, or ask for referrals in indie hacker communities

### Step 1.4 — Prepare Your NDA

Draft a mutual NDA before any buyer conversation. Key clauses:

- Non-disclosure of all technical details, financial data, user data
- Non-solicitation of your users or employees for 2 years
- Non-compete (light — just prohibits using your IP directly)
- Jurisdiction: your home state
- 3-year term

Use a lawyer or download a SaaS-specific NDA template from:
- Clerky (clerky.com) — startup legal docs
- Bonterms (bonterms.com) — open-source tech contracts
- LegalZoom ($150–$300 for a custom NDA)

## PHASE 2: VALUATION & ASKING PRICE STRATEGY (WEEK 3–5)

### Step 2.1 — Know Your Real Number

Based on the financial projections in this data room:

| Your Situation | Recommended Ask |
|----------------|----------------|
| 0 subscribers, pure IP + code | $800K–$1.5M (replacement value) |
| 50–200 subscribers | $2M–$4M |
| 200–500 subscribers | $4M–$8M |
| 500–1,500 subscribers | $8M–$20M |
| 1,500–5,000 subscribers | $20M–$60M |
| 5,000+ subscribers | $60M–$250M+ (needs professional banker) |

### Step 2.2 — Anchor High

In M&A, you anchor high and let the buyer negotiate you down. Never anchor at your floor.

**Strategy:** Add 30–40% to your target price when setting the asking price. This gives you negotiating room and filters out low-ball buyers.

**Example:** If your target is $10M, ask for $13.5M. You have room to land at $10M while appearing to have "negotiated."

### Step 2.3 — Walk-Away Number

Set this privately:
- What is the minimum you will accept?
- What is your best alternative if you don't sell? (Keep running it, raise capital, etc.)
- Write this number down and lock it away. Do not share it with anyone.

## PHASE 4: THE SALES PROCESS (WEEKS 6–16)

### Step 4.1 — Non-Confidential Teaser First

Never send the full data room first. Send a teaser:
- Platform category and key differentiator (1 paragraph)
- Headline metrics (subscriber count, ARR, or "significant subscriber base")
- 3 bullet points of technology differentiation
- "Full details available under NDA"

### Step 4.2 — Execute NDA

Once the buyer expresses real interest:
1. Send your NDA (use your attorney's template)
2. Get it signed and countersigned
3. Only then send the full data room

### Step 4.3 — Management Presentation (The Demo)

This is your most important call. Prepare a 30-minute presentation:

**Recommended Structure:**
1. The problem (2 min): "Bettors have no reliable analytical tool"
2. The solution (3 min): "SORS MAXIMA — simulation-native intelligence"
3. Live demo (12 min): Show Command Center → Parlay → Life Changer Ticket → AI Analyst → Admin Suite
4. Market size (3 min): "$40B market, zero dominant analytics player"
5. Financials (5 min): Revenue, ARR, growth, projections
6. Why you / why now (2 min): Codebase scope, IP portfolio, head start
7. Deal mechanics (3 min): What's included, transition support

**Demo tips:**
- Always demo live, not slides — the product is impressive, show it
- Show the admin intelligence suite — most buyers will not expect this level of operator tooling
- Pull up the SimulationDepthIndicator showing "Overdrive Elite — 1,000,000 simulations" — this is a jaw-dropper
- Show the AI Analyst companion responding to a real pick question

### Step 4.4 — Letter of Intent (LOI)

After a successful demo, interested buyers will submit an LOI (non-binding expression of interest). An LOI includes:
- Proposed purchase price (or range)
- Deal structure (cash, stock, earnout)
- Exclusivity period (typically 30–60 days)
- Key conditions (due diligence, regulatory)

**Negotiate the LOI hard** — it's the most negotiable document. Once you sign an LOI, the price almost never goes up.

**Key LOI clauses to fight for:**
- Price: Push for all-cash upfront, or at minimum 70%+ cash at close
- Earnout: Minimize or eliminate — earnouts almost always pay less than promised
- Exclusivity: 30 days is standard; push back on anything over 45 days
- No-shop clause: Narrow the definition of who you can talk to
- Reps & warranties: Negotiate the scope carefully (your attorney handles this)

### Step 4.5 — Due Diligence

After LOI is signed, buyer will conduct due diligence. Expect:

**Technical DD (2–4 weeks)**
- Code review by buyer's engineers
- Architecture review
- Security audit
- Data quality assessment
- API dependency audit

**Financial DD (2–3 weeks)**
- Stripe data export (monthly revenue, subscriber count, churn)
- Bank statements
- API cost receipts
- Operating cost documentation

**Legal DD (2–4 weeks)**
- IP ownership documentation (copyright filings, trademark filings)
- Terms of Service / Privacy Policy review
- Any existing contracts with users, data providers, API vendors

**Be prepared:** DD is where deals die. Have everything documented and ready to share.

### Step 4.6 — Purchase Agreement

After DD, buyer's attorney drafts the Purchase Agreement. This is a complex legal document. **Do not sign without your attorney reviewing it.**

Key sections to negotiate:
- **Reps & Warranties:** What you're promising is true about the business. Narrow these to what you know for certain.
- **Indemnification:** Limit your indemnification obligation (cap it at the purchase price)
- **Escrow/Holdback:** Buyer may hold 10–15% for 12–18 months. Negotiate this down.
- **Earnout provisions:** If present, define milestones precisely. Vague earnout = no earnout.
- **Non-compete:** Expect a 2–3 year non-compete in the specific category (sports betting analytics SaaS). This is standard and typically reasonable.
- **Transition support:** 30–90 days is standard. You can negotiate compensation for this period.

### Step 4.7 — Close

- Funds wired to your account (or escrow)
- Domain transfer, Replit project transfer, GitHub repo transfer
- Introduction email to key relationships (API vendors, etc.)
- Transition begins

## PHASE 5: DEAL STRUCTURES TO KNOW

### Structure 1 — All-Cash Acquisition (Best for Owner)

**What it is:** Buyer pays the full agreed price in cash at closing.
**Best for:** Deals under $10M, strategic buyers with budget.
**Risk to you:** None. You get paid in full.
**Negotiation tip:** Offer a 5–10% price reduction vs. other structures in exchange for all-cash. Buyers will often take it.

### Structure 2 — Cash + Earnout

**What it is:** Portion upfront in cash, remainder paid if the business hits defined milestones (revenue, subscribers) after acquisition.
**Typical split:** 70–80% upfront, 20–30% earnout over 12–24 months.
**Risk to you:** If the acquirer doesn't support the business, you may never see the earnout.
**Negotiation tip:** If you must accept an earnout, tie milestones to metrics YOU control (platform uptime, feature delivery) not to metrics the buyer controls (marketing spend, subscriber growth).

### Structure 3 — Asset Purchase vs. Stock Purchase

**Asset Purchase:** Buyer acquires specific assets (code, IP, brand, domain, user list). Most common for early-stage businesses. Simpler legally.
**Stock Purchase:** Buyer acquires the legal entity. More complex, exposes buyer to any existing liabilities.
**For SORS MAXIMA:** If you operate as an LLC or corp, the buyer will likely prefer an asset purchase. Structure your business accordingly before selling.

### Structure 4 — Licensing / Revenue Share (Not a Sale)

**What it is:** You keep ownership but license the IP to a strategic partner for a monthly fee or revenue share.
**Best for:** If you believe in the platform's future but need cash flow.
**Risk:** You remain responsible for the product; the partner may not invest appropriately.
**When to use:** Only if your valuation expectations exceed what the market will pay in a full sale.

## PHASE 6: NEGOTIATION TACTICS

### The Power of Silence

After quoting your price, stop talking. The first person to speak after a price quote is usually the one who concedes. Silence is a negotiation weapon.

### Always Quote a Range

"We're looking at $8M–$12M depending on deal structure" is stronger than "$10M." It anchors high on the top and shows flexibility.

### Use the Data Room as Leverage

When a buyer tries to low-ball: "I appreciate that, but we have two other parties currently reviewing the data room. I'm not going to be able to accept that figure."

*If true, say it. If not true, you still have alternatives — running it yourself, raising capital — which is "the other party."*

### The Comparative Exit Argument

"Action Network sold to Better Collective for $240M. Their technology was editorial-based with no simulation engine. SORS MAXIMA runs 1 million Monte Carlo simulations per game. We believe the technology premium is justified."

### On Earnouts

"I built this platform. I know what it's capable of. If I had to take an earnout, it would mean I'm not confident in the deal structure — and if I'm not confident, neither should you be. Let's structure this as a clean all-cash transaction."

## PHASE 8: WHAT TO DO IF IT DOESN'T SELL

If the first round of outreach doesn't generate a deal:

1. **Recalibrate the price** — The market is always right about price. If no offers come at your ask, the price is too high.
2. **Build more traction** — 6 months of growth at even 100 subscribers dramatically increases the valuation and buyer interest.
3. **Pivot to raising capital** — A $150K–$500K pre-seed round can fund growth to the point where a much higher exit is achievable.
4. **Go to the next tier of buyers** — If sportsbooks said no, try media companies. If media companies said no, try data companies.
5. **Try a different structure** — A licensing deal or revenue-share partnership that doesn't require an outright acquisition may unlock value while you continue building.
