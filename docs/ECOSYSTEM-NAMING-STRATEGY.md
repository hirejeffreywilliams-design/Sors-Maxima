## PART ONE: FAMILY NAME OPTIONS

### The Naming Brief

The overarching family name must accomplish five simultaneous goals:

1. **Work as a holding company name** — credible to investors, clean in legal filings
2. **Work as an ecosystem brand** — memorable, pronounceable, emotionally resonant
3. **Work as a reveal name** — dramatic enough to make headlines when disclosed
4. **Imply multidimensionality** — suggest that this is bigger than any one category
5. **Connect, without overpowering, the child brands** — 4everacy, Sors Maxima, and the Nova family must still shine independently

Below are seven strong candidates, each developed with rationale, tone analysis, and strategic positioning.

---

### OPTION 1: OMNIVEX

**Core Concept:** *Omni* (Latin: all, everywhere, total) + *Vex* as a phonetic riff on *Vertex* (the topmost point) and *Vex* (a dynamic, electrifying sound). The result: a name that feels like the apex of everything.

**Full Name in Context:**
- Holding Company: Omnivex Holdings
- Ecosystem Brand: The Omnivex Ecosystem
- Reveal Line: *"Everything was always Omnivex."*

**Why It Works:**
The *Omni-* prefix is already embedded in OmniDLOS — it is the founder's linguistic DNA. Omnivex extends that lineage while shedding the technical DLOS acronym. The *-vex* suffix punches hard phonetically: it is sharp, modern, and unlike anything else in the enterprise tech landscape. It does not sound like a startup. It sounds like an institution.

The word also subconsciously rhymes with *complex* — nodding to the intricate engineering beneath the surface — and *apex* — the summit. Together, the connotations stack: *total, highest point, interconnected*.

**Domain / Ticker Potential:** omnivex.com, OMNX  
**Tone:** Authoritative, architectural, permanent  
**Risk Level:** Low — distinctive coinage unlikely to conflict with existing marks

## PART TWO: SUB-BRAND NAMING AUDIT

### 2.1 Analysis of the Current Naming Architecture

The 13 platforms currently operate under three distinct naming conventions:

| Convention | Platforms | Count |
|---|---|---|
| Nova-prefix | NovaShield, TradeNova, NovaMusic, Nova-Holistic-Health, NovaRivals, Nova-AutismConnect, Nova-AutoCare, Nova-EventFamily, Nova-ProjectHub, Nova-SurvivalGuide | 10 |
| Standalone coined | 4everacy, Sors Maxima | 2 |
| Standalone descriptive | Tree-AI | 1 |

This creates a de facto family: the Nova constellation, with 4everacy as the mothership operating above it and Sors Maxima as a prestigious standalone entity.

---

### 2.2 Strengths of the Current "Nova-" Convention

**1. Immediate family signaling.** Nova creates instant visual and verbal cohesion across 10 platforms. A user who encounters NovaShield and later discovers NovaRivals immediately senses a relationship — even before the grand reveal.

**2. Semantic richness.** *Nova* (Latin: new; Astronomy: a star that dramatically increases in luminosity) carries extraordinary meaning. Every platform is a new star. The ecosystem is a galaxy. The metaphor is built-in.

**3. Flexibility.** Nova can precede or follow the category descriptor (NovaShield vs. TradeNova), giving naming teams room to maneuver based on what sounds better for each category.

**4. Memorability.** Two syllables, ending in an open vowel — *NO-vah* — is among the most memorable phonetic structures in naming science. It sits comfortably in the mouth and the memory.

**5. Globalness.** Nova is understood and positive in most major markets. It does not carry negative connotations in Spanish, Portuguese, French, German, or Mandarin market contexts.

---

### 2.3 Weaknesses of the Current "Nova-" Convention

**1. Crowding and confusion at scale.** Ten platforms sharing a Nova prefix will create genuine user confusion as the ecosystem scales. Which Nova is the music one? Which is the health one? This confusion is manageable now but will become a real support and marketing burden at 100,000+ combined users.

**2. Inconsistency signals incompletion.** The mix of Nova-prefix, standalone coins (4everacy, Sors Maxima), and descriptive names (Tree-AI) communicates that the naming was done iteratively, not architecturally. For the grand reveal to land with impact, there must be a coherent naming logic that the press and public can immediately grasp.

**3. Trademarked landscape congestion.** *Nova* is one of the most litigated words in trademark law — Nova Entertainment, Nova Networks, Nova Health, Nova Capital, and hundreds of others exist across Class 9 and Class 42. Each Nova-named platform requires its own clearance, and composite availability varies widely.

**4. The prefix creates a hierarchy problem.** Nova-prefix brands read as *products of Nova*, not as independent businesses. This undercuts the reveal strategy, which requires that each platform stand tall as a standalone entity whose ultimate connection to the parent is the surprise.

**5. Compound words with hyphens age poorly.** *Nova-AutismConnect*, *Nova-Holistic-Health*, and *Nova-EventFamily* are difficult to present in uppercase logos, on app store listings with character limits, and in verbal brand communication ("download Nova-Holistic-Health" is a mouthful).

---

### 2.4 Refinement Recommendations

**Short-term (Pre-Reveal):** Retain all existing names. Do not rebrand any platform now — the fragmentation is actually useful pre-reveal, as it prevents the pattern from being obvious. However, begin standardizing the written presentation:

- Remove hyphens from sub-brand names where possible: **NovaAutismConnect**, **NovaHolisticHealth**, **NovaEventFamily**, **NovaAutocare**, **NovaSurvivalGuide**, **NovaProjectHub**
- Capitalize consistently: the Nova prefix should always be capitalized, but the word following should also be title-cased, not hyphen-separated

**Medium-term (Reveal Phase):** Upon ecosystem reveal, refresh the sub-brand naming with cleaner, more distinctive names that retain family connection through a shared suffix or design system rather than a shared prefix. Suggested convention: the **-Is** or **-All** or **-Eon** suffix family.

| Current Name | Refined Option A | Refined Option B |
|---|---|---|
| NovaShield | ShieldAxis | Sentis |
| TradeNova | TradeAxis | TradeEon |
| NovaMusic | SoundAxis | SoundEon |
| Nova-Holistic-Health | WellAxis | Welleon |
| NovaRivals | RivalsAxis | ArenaEon |
| Nova-AutismConnect | BridgeAxis | ConnectEon |
| Nova-AutoCare | CarAxis | AutoEon |
| Nova-EventFamily | GatherAxis | CeremEon |
| Nova-ProjectHub | BuildAxis | ProjectEon |
| Nova-SurvivalGuide | PrepAxis | SurvivalEon |

This is illustrative. The decision to rename sub-brands should be deferred until the family name is locked, so the suffix or connector element can be derived from the parent brand's phonetic identity.

### 2.5 Naming Hierarchy Framework

The recommended four-tier naming architecture:

```
TIER 1: HOLDING COMPANY
[Family Name] Holdings, Inc.
Example: Omnivex Holdings, Inc.

TIER 2: ECOSYSTEM BRAND
[Family Name] — the consumer-facing name of the unified ecosystem
Example: "The Omnivex Ecosystem" / "Powered by Omnivex"

TIER 3: PLATFORM BRAND
Each of the 13 named platforms
Example: 4everacy | Sors Maxima | NovaShield | TradeNova...

TIER 4: FEATURE / ENGINE BRAND
Proprietary features and engines within platforms
Example: Sports Button (46-Factor Quantum Fusion Engine) | DLOS Core | QuickScan | QuantEngine
```

This hierarchy allows each layer to operate independently in market communications while remaining architecturally connected. The investor deck leads with Tier 1. Consumer marketing leads with Tier 3. Press releases about the reveal bridge Tiers 1 and 3. Feature launches operate at Tier 4.

## PART FOUR: THE REVEAL STRATEGY

### 4.1 The Strategic Context of the Reveal

This is not a normal product launch. This is the unmasking of an empire that was built in public, hiding in plain sight.

The reveal must accomplish three things simultaneously:

1. **Shock** — the world must be genuinely surprised that 13 separate-seeming platforms are one family
2. **Reverence** — the depth of vision and engineering must be communicated in a way that commands respect, not skepticism
3. **Invitation** — every person who hears the reveal must feel they have been personally invited into something larger than themselves

The model is not Alphabet. It is not Meta. The closest analog in history is **the moment when audiences first learned that the Marvel Cinematic Universe was interconnected all along** — individual films that felt standalone were actually coordinated acts of a single narrative. The reveal was not a press conference. It was a feeling.

---

### 4.2 The Three-Phase Reveal Architecture

**PHASE 1: THE WHISPER (Months 1–3)**

Begin seeding the connection without announcing it. In platform updates, app store descriptions, and social bios, add a single, cryptic line:

*"Part of something larger."*

No links. No explanations. Just the phrase. Let curiosity do the work.

Simultaneously, register the holding company's consumer-facing brand name and launch a minimal holding page: `[FamilyName].com` — a black screen with the logo, 13 glowing dots, and the words: *"We've been here the whole time."*

**PHASE 2: THE SIGNAL (Month 4)**

Begin connecting the dots publicly but incompletely. A long-form founder article (published on LinkedIn, X/Twitter, and a dedicated Medium/Substack) titled:

*"I Built 13 Companies. They Were Always One."*

The piece does not name the holding company. It describes the vision — the 13 dimensions of human life — and invites readers to look at the platforms they may already use with new eyes. It ends: *"The name drops on [date]."*

This generates earned media, speculation, and anticipation without revealing the full picture. Press outlets will cover it. Newsletters will speculate. The platforms' existing user bases will become brand ambassadors.

**PHASE 3: THE REVEAL EVENT (Month 5)**

A single, high-production live stream event — the ecosystem's founder, on camera, walking through each of the 13 platforms, explaining the vision that connected them from the beginning. The reveal of the holding company name at the climax of the presentation. A simultaneous update to every platform's app store listing, website header, and social media profile: *"Part of [Family Name]."*

The dimensional wheel graphic deploys across all channels simultaneously. The visual coherence of 13 different brand colors converging into one icon is itself the story.

### 4.4 Maintaining Standalone Brand Identity Through the Reveal

The risk of a reveal is brand dilution: platforms that have built their own user trust may feel their identity subsumed by the parent. This is managed through the **Endorsed Architecture** model — each platform retains its full standalone identity, name, design system, and community, with only a single connection to the parent brand:

- A small, elegant **"Part of [Family Name]"** badge in the footer of each platform's website
- An app store description line: *"[Platform Name] — a [Family Name] company"*
- A brand story page within each platform: *"How [Platform Name] fits into the larger vision"*

The parent brand never overrides the child brand in consumer-facing contexts. It only *endorses* it. The child brand earns user trust independently. The parent brand earns investor and press credibility architecturally. Both win.

## PART FIVE: TRADEMARK CONSIDERATIONS

### 5.1 Overview of Trademark Strategy for Multi-Platform Ecosystems

A 13-platform ecosystem requires a three-layer trademark strategy:

1. **The parent mark** — the holding company / ecosystem brand name
2. **The platform marks** — each of the 13 platform names
3. **The feature marks** — proprietary engines, features, and slogans (Sports Button, 46-Factor Quantum Fusion Engine, DLOS, etc.)

Each layer requires independent clearance, application, and maintenance. The good news: the more distinctive the marks, the easier and cheaper they are to defend.

---

| Name | Distinctiveness Class | Likely Conflicts | Class 42 Availability Estimate | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Omnivex** | Fanciful (coined) | Low — no obvious existing marks | High | STRONG — file immediately |
| **Dimensia** | Suggestive | Low — no major tech conflicts found | High | STRONG — file immediately |

**Key Principle:** Fanciful or coined marks (Omnivex, Vaeltis) receive the broadest trademark protection because they have no prior meaning. Suggestive marks receive moderate protection. └── [remaining 11 platforms as above]

```
FEATURE MARKS (filed under holding company, used by platforms under license)
└── 46-Factor Quantum Fusion Engine, DLOS, etc.
```

**Jurisdiction Priority:**
1. United States (USPTO) — first and highest priority
2. European Union (EUIPO) — through Madrid Protocol, single application covers 27 member states
3. United Kingdom (UKIPO) — post-Brexit, separate from EU
4. Canada (CIPO) — given proximity and shared market
5. Australia (IP Australia) — high-tech adoption market

Total estimated trademark cost for full portfolio (all 13 marks + parent + features): **$35,000–$65,000 USD** over 24 months, depending on whether clearance searches reveal office actions requiring response.

## PART SIX: TOP 3 RECOMMENDATIONS

### RECOMMENDATION #1: OMNIVEX

**Why This Wins**

Omnivex is the strongest name available for this ecosystem because it solves every naming problem simultaneously:

- It is **fanciful** — coined from roots, not found in any dictionary, which means maximum trademark protection with minimum conflict risk
- It extends and dignifies the **OmniDLOS** legacy — the *Omni-* prefix is already part of the founder's vocabulary and the holding company's identity, making the transition feel evolutionary, not revolutionary
- It is **phonetically dominant** — five letters, two syllables, hard consonants at beginning and end; it commands space in a sentence
- It has **institutional gravitas** — it does not sound like a startup; it sounds like something that has existed for decades and will exist for decades more
- The *-vex* suffix creates **electrifying connotations**: vertex, vortex, convex — all suggesting a top point, a convergence, a center of energy

**Strategic Notes:**
The transition from *OmniDLOS Holdings* to *Omnivex Holdings* is the smoothest available — existing legal entities, domain portfolios, and institutional relationships can reference the *Omni-* thread. Investors who knew the company as OmniDLOS will immediately connect the dots. New investors and press will encounter a fresh, powerful name with no baggage.

### RECOMMENDATION #2: VAELTIS

**Why This Is Second**

Vaeltis is the most distinctive name on the list — it is linguistically original, phonetically strong, and entirely uncrowded in trademark databases. Its Finnish etymology (*to traverse widely, to journey across all terrain*) is a perfect metaphor for an ecosystem that covers every dimension of human life.

The name also has a quality rare in brand naming: **it grows in meaning.** On first hearing, Vaeltis is interesting but opaque. Upon learning its meaning — *the act of traversing all of human existence* — it becomes profound. This layered disclosure mirrors the ecosystem reveal strategy perfectly: something that seems small and contained, revealed to be vast and deep.

This is not a fatal flaw (Alphabet, Accenture, and Spotify required the same education), but it means more creative work in the reveal moment to make the name land with meaning. The payoff of that work is a name no competitor can ever approach, and no dictionary will ever define away.

### RECOMMENDATION #3: DIMENSIA

**Why This Closes the Top Three**

Dimensia is the most strategically resonant name for a 13-platform ecosystem built on the explicit premise that each platform represents a *dimension* of human life. The name does real intellectual work: it tells the story of the structure without requiring a press release to explain it.

The phonetic near-homophone with *dementia* is managed, not avoided. The spelling tells the corrected story immediately. And in a media landscape where memorable names drive headline coverage, the slight provocative tension of *Dimensia* will generate more earned media discussion than a purely safe name.

## APPENDIX B: INVESTOR NARRATIVE FRAMEWORK

When presenting to investors — whether Series A, strategic partners, or acquisition conversations — the following narrative arc is recommended:

**Opening Statement:**
*"Most technology companies build one product and call it a platform. We built 13 platforms and called them companies. Today I want to show you what they actually are."*

**The Setup:**
Walk through each of the 13 platforms individually. Present each as a standalone business with its own TAM, unit economics, and user base. Do not connect them. Let each one stand.

**The Pivot:**
*"What you have just seen is not 13 companies. What you have just seen is 13 dimensions of human life, all built by one engineering team, all powered by shared infrastructure, all orchestrated from a single vision that began [founding year]. Welcome to [Family Name]."*

**The Close:**
*"You did not invest in one platform. You invested in every dimension of what a human being needs to live, compete, heal, build, protect, play, and thrive. That is what [Family Name] is. That is what we have always been."*
